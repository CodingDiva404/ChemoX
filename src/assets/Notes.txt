Experiment 1: Determining the Boiling Point of Water
Methodology and Procedure
Setup: Use a clean beaker and fill it with distilled water. Place it on a tripod stand and heat it using a Bunsen burner.
Observation: Record the temperature at regular intervals until the water starts boiling.
Analysis: Note the constant temperature when the water boils and compare it with the standard boiling point of water (100°C).
Explanation of Scientific Principles
The boiling point of water is the temperature at which it transitions from a liquid to a gas. This experiment illustrates the concept of phase changes and the properties of water.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them
Incorrect Temperature Recording: Ensure the thermometer is placed correctly in the water without touching the beaker’s sides.
Contaminated Water: Use distilled water to avoid impurities affecting the boiling point.
Real-World Applications
Understanding boiling points is crucial in various industries, including food processing and chemical manufacturing.

Experiment 2: Observing Plant Cell Structure Under a Microscope
Methodology and Procedure
Preparation: Take a fresh leaf and cut a thin slice to prepare a wet mount slide.
Observation: Use a microscope to observe the plant cells and identify the cell wall, nucleus, and chloroplasts.
Analysis: Draw and label the observed structures.
Explanation of Scientific Principles
This experiment helps students understand cell structure and function, highlighting the differences between plant and animal cells.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them
Improper Slide Preparation: Ensure the slice is thin enough for light to pass through easily.
Incorrect Focus: Adjust the microscope properly to get a clear view of the cells.
Real-World Applications
Knowledge of cell structure is fundamental in fields such as botany, genetics, and biotechnology.
